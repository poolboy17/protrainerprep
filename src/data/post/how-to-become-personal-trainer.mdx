## Step 1: Make Sure You're Eligible

Every legitimate certification requires two things before you can sit for the exam:

1. **A high school diploma or GED**
2. **Current CPR/AED certification**

That's it. No college degree required. No prerequisite courses. No prior work experience.

The CPR/AED certification costs $25–$75 and can be completed in a few hours through the American Red Cross, American Heart Association, or similar providers. Some organizations offer online options with an in-person skills check.

## Step 2: Choose Your Certification

This is where most people get stuck. There are over a dozen personal trainer certifications (covered in our [Fitness Certification Guide](/blog/fitness-certification-guide)) available, but only a handful matter for employment. The key filter: **NCCA accreditation**.

The National Commission for Certifying Agencies (NCCA) accredits certification programs that meet rigorous standards for exam development, administration, and maintenance. Major gym chains require — or strongly prefer — NCCA-accredited certifications.

## Step 3: Study for the Exam

Most certification programs are self-paced, meaning you study on your own schedule. Expect to spend **8–16 weeks studying 5–10 hours per week**, depending on your science background and study habits.

**What you'll learn across all major certifications:**
- Human anatomy and physiology (muscles, joints, movement patterns)
- Exercise science (energy systems, biomechanics, adaptations)
- Program design (sets, reps, periodization, progression)
- Nutrition fundamentals (macronutrients, energy balance, basic guidelines)
- Client assessment (fitness testing, health screening, movement analysis)
- Safety and risk management (contraindications, emergency procedures)

## Step 4: Pass the Exam

All major certification exams are computer-based and administered at testing centers (Pearson VUE, Prometric, or PSI) or via online proctoring. ISSA is the exception, offering an open-book option.

**Typical exam structure:**
- 120–160 multiple-choice questions
- 2–3 hour time limit
- Scaled passing score around 70%
- Results usually within 24–72 hours

Pass rates vary by certification — from NASM's ~64% to ACE's ~72%. These aren't high-stakes medical boards, but they're not guaranteed passes either. Study seriously.

## Step 5: Get Hired

With certification in hand, you have several employment paths (if you're [switching careers specifically](/blog/best-cert-career-changers), see our dedicated guide):

**Commercial gym (most common first job):** Chains like LA Fitness, 24 Hour Fitness, Gold's Gym, and Planet Fitness hire entry-level trainers. Expect a base pay of $10–$15/hour for floor time plus 40–60% commission on training sessions. Total first-year income typically ranges from $28,000–$38,000.

**Premium gym:** Equinox, Lifetime Fitness, and boutique studios pay more but hire selectively. Expect $18–$30/hour with stronger commission structures. These often prefer NASM or ACE certifications.

**Independent training:** Working for yourself — either in-home, at a rented studio space, or online. Higher earning potential but requires business skills and client acquisition. Most trainers build toward this after 1–3 years of gym experience.
